## LAYER 2: Inferred Functional Expertise (no fixed cap — quality over quantity)

**Layer 2 is INFERENCE ONLY. It must never contain text copied from the profile.**

The test for each candidate is: *"Did I read this label in the profile, or did I deduce it from what the profile implies?"* If you read it — it is explicit. Explicit items belong in Layer 1, Layer 1.5, or Layer 3. Only genuinely deduced capabilities belong in Layer 2.

**Core principle: include every inference that genuinely qualifies. Do NOT stop at an arbitrary number, and do NOT add items to reach a threshold.** A senior professional with 20 years of diverse experience may legitimately have 8–10 inferred capabilities; a narrow specialist may have 2. Both are correct. The only gate is genuine deduction — not count.

Layer 2 is split into two tiers with separate sourcing rules.

### Tier B — Evidence-Based Inferences

Evidence-based inferences are capabilities deduced from concrete profile content — specific phrases in the bio, experience, quantifiable results, or industry-specific context that imply a functional capability not declared in L1 or L1.5.

**MANDATORY SELF-CHECK — run this exact procedure for every Tier B candidate:**

1. Search for the candidate phrase (or its core compound words) in **declared fields only**: `linkedin_headline`, `linkedin_summary`, `linkedin_skills`, `website_capabilities`.

   **Do NOT search `bio`, `title`, `department`, or `linkedin_experience` for this check.** Those are narrative fields that describe what was done — they are sources of inference triggers, not capability declarations. A phrase appearing in a career narrative does not make it explicit; it makes it a strong trigger for inference.

2. **If the phrase is ABSENT from all declared fields** → it may qualify for Tier B (proceed to condition checks below).

3. **If the phrase IS found in a declared field** (exact, reordered, compound words present, synonym, or skill tag):
   - Ask: *"Is this candidate a meaningfully more specific technique or sub-activity, derived from concrete evidence — NOT just a decorated version of what's in the declared field?"*
   - **If YES (genuine elaboration)** → it may still qualify for Tier B. See elaboration test below.
   - **If NO (copy or near-copy)** → STOP. Route to Layer 1.5 (if declared capability) or Layer 3 (if sub-topic).

**What counts as "found" in a declared field:**
- Exact: `"Market Access Strategy"` in `linkedin_summary` → found
- Reordering: `"Strategy for Market Access"` in `linkedin_summary` → found
- Core **compound** words at the same abstraction level: declared field has `"pricing strategy"` → `"Pricing Strategy"` → found (same concept). **Single generic words do not count** — `"strategy"`, `"growth"`, `"operations"`, `"finance"` appearing anywhere do NOT block a candidate; only domain-specific compound phrases do.
- Synonym: declared field has `"go-to-market"` → `"Go-to-Market Strategy"` → found
- Skill tag: `"Pricing Strategy"` in `linkedin_skills` → found

**Elaboration test — a candidate passes only if BOTH hold:**
- The profile contains a broader term, not the specific technique itself
- The candidate is a concrete, more specific technique directly implied by the evidence — not just adding a modifier to the found phrase

**Elaboration examples:**
- Profile: `"pricing work"` (broad) → candidate: `"Value-Based Pricing Implementation"` ✅ passes — concrete technique not stated in profile
- Profile: `"pricing strategy"` (same level) → candidate: `"Pricing Strategy"` ❌ fails — same concept, verbatim blocked
- Profile: `"M&A advisory"` (broad) → candidate: `"Carve-out Execution"` ✅ passes — specific sub-activity deduced from context
- Profile: `"due diligence"` → candidate: `"Commercial Due Diligence"` ❌ fails — mere decoration, same abstraction level

**Wrong/Correct examples:**
- ❌ WRONG: profile says "Market Access" in skills → Layer 2: `"Market Access Strategy"` — copied with decoration
- ✅ CORRECT: profile says "launched oncology product in 3 EU markets" → Layer 2: `"Market Access Strategy"` — genuinely deduced from launch evidence
- ❌ WRONG: profile says "post-merger integration" → Layer 2: `"Post-Merger Integration"` — verbatim
- ✅ CORRECT: profile describes "led 4 acquisitions at PE-backed firm, overseeing day-1 readiness" → Layer 2: `"Post-Merger Integration"` — deduced from acquisition leadership evidence

**Confidence threshold for Tier B:** include if ANY ONE of the following holds strongly:
- Specific project or engagement evidence in bio/experience (e.g., "led carve-out of manufacturing division") — this alone is sufficient
- Quantifiable result that implies the capability (e.g., "improved EBITDA by 30%" → EBITDA Improvement Programs)
- Industry-standard role expectation tightly tied to a named function (e.g., "Head of FP&A" → Budgeting & Forecasting)

Seniority alone is NOT sufficient for Tier B (that is Tier A's domain). Tier B requires concrete evidence from the narrative.

## SECTORS & MATCHED_SECTORS

### Core principle
Assign a sector when full profile context makes it clear the person works with clients in that industry — the sector name need not appear explicitly. Use client names, project descriptions, industry terminology, role titles, practice group names, and website fields.

Examples: "NHS contract negotiations" → Healthcare; "advised on J.P. Morgan's acquisition of First Republic" → Financial Services; "EBITDA expansion at a national grocery chain" → Consumer & Retail.

### Adjacency mistakes to avoid
- **Data centers / digital infrastructure** → Technology, NOT Real Estate (only assign Real Estate when profile explicitly references property transactions, development, asset management, or property-sector clients)
- **Food & Beverage** → NOT Consumer & Retail (requires explicit retail stores, apparel, electronics, or non-food consumer categories)
- **Pharmaceuticals** → NOT Healthcare (requires explicit hospitals, clinics, providers, or payers)
- **Automotive** → NOT Industrials & Manufacturing (requires explicit manufacturing or heavy-industry evidence beyond automotive)

### Disambiguation when multiple matched_sectors strings fit the same evidence
Prefer in order:

1. **Most specific match within the same sector cluster** — apply this only when both strings describe the same underlying industry. Example: "Pharmaceutical" over "Life Sciences" when all evidence is drug-specific; "Life Sciences" over "Pharmaceutical" when work spans biotech, diagnostics, and devices. Do NOT let specificity override frequency across different sector families.
2. **Most frequently evidenced** in the profile — use this when strings represent genuinely different industries, or when the specificity tiebreaker does not apply (e.g., "Life Sciences" 5x vs "Pharmaceutical" 1x → frequency wins regardless of specificity).
3. **Alphabetically first** as deterministic fallback when frequency is also tied.

Include multiple strings only when distinct evidence supports each.

### `sectors` (free text)
Each entry is a **distinct top-level industry** — not a sub-specialization or topic.

**Consolidation rule:** merge multiple types of work within the same industry into a single entry.
- ❌ Wrong: `["education policy", "education technology", "k-12 education"]` (one industry)
- ✅ Correct: `["education"]`
- ✅ Correct: `["healthcare", "financial services"]` (genuinely distinct industries)

Use short plain industry names (1–3 words). If a `sectors` entry has no match in `<matched_sectors>`, include the free-text entry only.

### `matched_sectors` (controlled vocabulary)
Use exact strings from the `<matched_sectors>` list. Do NOT modify them. Apply the disambiguation hierarchy when multiple strings fit the same evidence.

**CRITICAL — vocabulary mixing:** `<sectors>` and `<matched_sectors>` are TWO SEPARATE lists with different strings. A value that appears in `<sectors>` (e.g. "Technology & Software", "Financial Services") is NEVER valid in `matched_sectors`. A value from the profile text (e.g. "Enterprise Software", "SaaS", "Cloud Computing") is NEVER valid in `matched_sectors` unless it appears verbatim in `<matched_sectors>`.

**Technology/software mapping guide** (common source of errors):
- Evidence of software, SaaS, enterprise tech, IT, cloud, or digital platforms → `Computing, Technology, Robotics & AI` (exact string in `<matched_sectors>`)
- Evidence of hardware, semiconductors, electronics manufacturing → `Electronics & Electrical`
- Evidence of telecom networks, carriers, ISPs → `Telecommunications`
- "Enterprise Software", "Technology & Software", "Technology", "Computing", "SaaS", "Cloud" are NOT valid `<matched_sectors>` strings — use `Computing, Technology, Robotics & AI` instead

### When to use `Agnostic`

`Agnostic` means the profile provides **no usable sector signal** — the work is genuinely industry-neutral and no specific client industry can be identified anywhere in the full profile.

**`Agnostic` is a last resort, not a hedge.** Do NOT assign it when:
- The bio uses generic scope language — "across multiple industries", "cross-sector", "diverse sectors", "multiple jurisdictions", "broad range of clients" — while **also** naming specific industries. Generic scope phrases describe breadth, not the absence of sector identity. Extract the named sectors and discard the generic language.
- The person's functional expertise is broad (e.g. general management, HR, finance) but they still served identifiable client industries.

**Only assign `Agnostic` when ALL of the following hold:**
- No specific client industry is named or implied anywhere in the profile (bio, title, LinkedIn, website fields, practice group names, client names)
- No industry-specific terminology provides a sector signal
- The work is genuinely function-only with no detectable industry tilt

**Examples:**
- ❌ Wrong: bio says "advised clients across financial services, healthcare, and energy on operational transformation" → assign `financial services`, `healthcare`, `energy & utilities` — NOT Agnostic
- ❌ Wrong: bio says "worked across multiple industries" but practice group is "Healthcare & Life Sciences" → assign `healthcare` — NOT Agnostic
- ✅ Correct: bio says only "helps leadership teams improve organisational performance" with zero industry signal anywhere → Agnostic

## PRIMARY EXPERTISE
The category that best represents the person's deepest experience and current professional identity. Must be an EXACT string from `<expertise>`. **Sector names (e.g. "Pharmaceuticals & Life Sciences", "Healthcare", "Financial Services", "Technology & Software") are INVALID here — they belong in `sectors`, not `primary_expertise`.**

**Tiebreak — stop at the FIRST rule that applies:**

0. **Title/practice names MULTIPLE categories** (e.g., "Partner, M&A and Technology Practice"): pick the category with most supporting evidence elsewhere; if still tied, use order in the title (first-named wins).

A "Global TMT Sector Lead" IS the Technology expert at that firm; their identity and career are built around that domain regardless of what functional work (growth strategy, M&A, etc.) they perform within it. Map the named domain to an EXACT or UNAMBIGUOUS `<expertise>` string and STOP — do NOT read the bio or apply frequency signals afterward. **CRITICAL: do NOT use "closest match" logic.      - ✅ CORRECT: "Associate Director, Global Health" → Global Health is a sector/programme → skip to Rule 3 → bio shows operational improvement work → primary = `Operational Improvements`, justification cites Rule 3 and bio evidence
     - ✅ CORRECT: "Managing Director, Healthcare Practice" → Healthcare is a sector → skip to Rule 3 → bio shows M&A deal advisory → primary = `M&A and Corporate Development`, justification cites Rule 3 and the bio evidence

2. **Current practice group or team name identifies a single category** → use that category.

For every assigned item, record which input field(s) provided supporting evidence.

### Source normalization
Map input field labels to these EXACT keys:

| Input label              | `source` key            |
|--------------------------|-------------------------|
| `Bio:`                   | `bio`                   |
| `Title:`                 | `title`                 |
| `Department:`            | `department`            |

### Keys
- `categories` → `explicit_expertise_13`
- `declared_capabilities` → `declared_narrow_capabilities`
- `sectors` → `sectors`
- `matched_sectors` → `matched_sectors`
- `inferred` → `inferred_expertise_functional`
- `topics` → `topic_overlap`

Each value: list of `{"source": "<key>", "text": "<phrase>"}` objects.

**Text length:** typically ≤15 words, up to 25 if needed to preserve semantic context.

**Multi-category evidence:** the same evidence may legitimately appear under multiple categories when one phrase supports several inferences.

### Evidence semantics differ by layer

**L1, L1.5, sectors, matched_sectors, topics** — `text` is a direct quote from the profile that *contains or names* the item. The profile explicitly states the capability or sector.

**L2 (`inferred`)** — `text` is the **inference trigger**: a profile phrase that *implies* the capability, not one that states it. The inferred item must be ABSENT from the profile (that is what the self-check verifies); the evidence is the reasoning chain input — what you saw that led you to deduce it.

- ✅ Correct: inferred `"Post-Merger Integration"` → `{"source": "bio", "text": "led 4 acquisitions at PE-backed firm, overseeing day-1 readiness"}` — the trigger, not a mention of the capability
- ❌ Wrong: inferred `"Post-Merger Integration"` → `{"source": "bio", "text": "post-merger integration work"}` — if this phrase exists in the profile, the item fails the self-check and must not be in Layer 2 at all

**Role-standard inferences** (e.g., "CFO" → "Financial Planning & Analysis") may cite the title or seniority signal as the sole trigger when no single phrase is more specific: `{"source": "title", "text": "CFO, genomics scale-up"}`. This is expected and correct — the inference is justified by role expectation, not by a matching phrase.

Omit a key entirely if its array is empty.
